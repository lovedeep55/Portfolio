Mechanical Design of an Electric Motor Mount and Coupling System

Project Overview

This project involved the design and development of motor mounts and a coupler for a high-performance system operating at 15,000 RPM and 410 Nm of torque. The gearbox cover and internal gears were removed, and the entire system was mounted for testing. The purpose of this setup was to evaluate the motor's performance on a dynamometer.

Responsibilities

  • Designed detailed CAD models and technical drawings for the motor mounts, coupler, and adapter.
  • Developed an integrated cooling and lubrication system for the motor and bearings.
  • Selected off-the-shelf components such as bearings and seals to meet performance and reliability requirements.
  • Manufacturing and assembly of the motor and couplers.

Bearing and Sealing challenge

  • Situation & Task:
    • The first challenge in designing a system to operate at 15,000 RPM was selecting appropriate bearings and seals.
    • I designed the initial iteration of the motor coupler (55 mm diameter) to accommodate both the bearing and the seal. However, the lead times for these components exceeded the project timeline (as shown in the first image).
    • Another issue with this design was the complexity of assembly and disassembly. In the event of seal failure, replacing the seal would be difficult.
  • Action:
    • Most bearings capable of operating at 15,000 RPM are designed for shafts with diameters smaller than 35–40 mm. This was my first key insight after researching readily available bearing options.
    • To accommodate an off-the-shelf bearing and seal rated for 15,000 RPM, I redesigned the shaft to step down from 60 mm to 35 mm in diameter.
    • Additionally, I incorporated an adaptor on the opposite end of the shaft to interface with the dynamometer coupler.
  • Result:
    • The shaft and bearing assembly is user-friendly, allowing for easy integration with the motor and ensuring concentric alignment on the mounting.

Cooling and Lubrication

  • Situation & Task:
    • The next challenge was to design a cooling and lubrication system for the motor and bearings, as the original gears—previously responsible for oil splash and circulation within the motor—had been removed.
    • While adding an external oil pump was straightforward, the main challenge was creating a closed-loop oil circuit within the motor, as there was no proper inlet available. Additionally, ensuring effective lubrication for the bearing installed on the shaft added to the complexity.
  • Action:
    • I figured out the internal cooling loop of the motor, and the make custom inlet to feed oil inside the motor.
    • For bearing i added a addition oil inlet in the bearing holder. This way i was able to solve the coolign and lubrication challenge
  • Result:
    • The shaft and bearing were easy to assemble and are now ready for integration with the motor mount.

Assembly challenge

  • Situation & Task:
    • The first challenge was the misalignment between the shaft and pinion center axes caused by welding. This issue was critical, especially when the system operated at 15,000 RPM.
    • The second problem arose from the lack of accurate CAD data for the motor shaft. Due to rough estimations, the bearing assembly does not sit flush with the mount, resulting in a 3 mm gap (see image below).
  • Action:
    • The shaft misalignment issue was resolved by checking runouts and machining specific holes to meet the 2.5 G balancing requirement.
    • The gap issue was addressed by fabricating a 3.5 mm shim disk to accommodate the mismatch. While this introduced additional potential seal failure points, it served as a quick solution for testing purposes.
  • Result:
    • The system is currently in the testing phase, operating at low cooling pressure and reduced RPM.
