Mechanical Design of an Electric Motor Mount and Coupling System

Project Overview

This project focused on the design and development of motor mounts and a coupler for a high-performance system operating at 15,000 RPM with a torque of 450 Nm. The gearbox was removed, and the motor mount was integrated directly onto the existing gearbox casing.

Responsibilities

  • Designed detailed CAD models and technical drawings for the motor mounts, coupler, and adapter.
  • Developed an integrated cooling and lubrication system for the motor and bearings.
  • Selected off-the-shelf components such as bearings and seals to meet performance and reliability requirements.
  • Manufacturing and assembly of the motor and couplers.

Component selection

  • Situation & Task:
    • This was Cellprop's first electric motor design project, still in its early development phase. As the Mechanical Engineering Intern, I was responsible for defining thermal system requirements, identifying potential thermal failure points to support the thermal design process.
  • Action:
    • A literature review revealed that elevated temperatures can lead to insulation failure, ultimately resulting in motor failure. Therefore, the maximum allowable temperature near the insulation is determined by its thermal class.
    • It was found that for every 10°C increase in temperature, the lifespan of the insulation is reduced by half.
  • Result:
    • I determined the temperature limit for the copper winding to be 120 °C, based on Class-H insulation specifications.

Heat load estimation

  • Task:
    • After identifying the thermal requirements and critical failure points, my next objective was to analyze heat generation and determine the resulting temperature distribution within the motor.
  • Action:
    • Calculated the heat load from copper losses in both AC and DC windings based on current flow.
    • Estimated core losses in the stator and rotor laminations using frequency and magnetic flux density values.
  • Result:
    • The total heat generated in the copper windings was approximately 55 watts.
    • Core losses were estimated at 61 watts in the stator and 20 watts in the rotor, based on an operating frequency of 100 Hz and a flux density of 15 kilogauss.
    • Core losses were estimated at 15 watts in the stator and 5 watts in the rotor, based on an operating frequency of 50 Hz and a flux density of 15 kilogauss.

Thermal Simulations

  • Task:
    • Following the estimation of the heat load, my subsequent objective was to conduct a detailed analysis of the internal temperature distribution and determine an effective thermal management strategy for the motor.
  • Action:
    • My initial step was to assess whether natural convection would suffice or if forced air convection was necessary. Based on the total heat dissipation, I calculated the surface area required to support natural convection.
    • Estimated the total thermal resistance from the copper windings to the motor’s external surface, excluding fins and assuming natural convection. This served as the baseline case for subsequent design development.
    • Completed simulations for the motor in Siemens FEMAP(TMG).
    • I calculated the equivalent thermal conductivity of the copper (considering the varnish) and steel laminations— considering the laminated stack structure—in both the axial and radial directions.
  • Result:
    • Based on the calculations and simulation results, it was concluded that the motor could be adequately cooled using natural convection at an operating frequency of 50 Hz.

Manufacturing and Assembly

  • Task:
    • Once the motor's mechanical performance and thermal management were validated, the next step was to proceed with its manufacturing.
  • Action:
    • For the stator and rotor laminates, i worked with local vendor to Laser cut it.
    • For the motor casing, we used an off-the-shelf pipe and performed a pipe boring operation to achieve the required internal dimensions. This approach was chosen to reduce manufacturing costs.
  • Result:
    • I successfully manufactured the stator, rotor, and casing using local vendors. The manufacturing process was completed within the allocated budget and timeline.
