Mechanical design of 1.2kW Charger for Ultraviolette automotive

Project Overview

This project was a customer-driven initiative aimed at developing a 1.2kW prototype charger to demonstrate the bike's charging capabilities during an investor showcase meeting. It was not a production version, but rather a prototype for investors. The project faced constraints related to limited space and airflow for inlets and outlets, and Exro was working with a restricted budget.

Responsibilities

  • Detailed CAD modeling of the charger components.
  • Ensuring thermal and electrical safety of the charger.
  • Collaborating with the electrical engineering team to develop the layout of the charger.
  • Conducting simulations and testing for performance verification.
  • Managing project timelines and delivery milestones.

Charger Casing Manufacturing Cost

  • Situation:
    • The CNC machining cost for the aluminum block used in the casing exceeded the budget. Exro was in a tight financial situation, making it clear that machining the casing was not a feasible option.
  • Task:
    • As the mechanical engineer for this project, I was tasked with finding a way to reduce costs while maintaining the design's integrity.
  • Action:
    • The solution to this problem was to machine each side of the casing separately.
    • Complete welding was performed on all four sides of the casing.
  • Result:
    • By welding all four sides of the casing, I was able to reduce the cost of the charger casing by 40%.

Thermal Design Challenges

  • Situation:
    • A height constraint for the charger casing limited the selection of fans for optimal airflow.
    • Due to fixed ducting, the airflow inlet was positioned at the front, and the outlet was on the side of the charger, complicating the airflow path.
    • Given these constraints, the situation posed an optimization challenge.
  • Task:
    • Considering the space limitations and a heat loss of 80-90 watts, I was tasked with designing a thermal system for the charger.
  • Action:
    • To address this, I selected a fan with a maximum height of 55mm, which provided the highest possible CFM.
    • Based on forced convection calculations (h-value), I determined the necessary number of fins for effective heat transfer.
    • Several simulations were conducted in Mentor FloTHERM to optimize the number and height of the fins.
  • Result:
    • For thermal testing, I incorporated resistors to simulate various heat load profiles
    • The charger was able to operate at 90% of its rated load. However, there were some issues with airflow patterns and choking.
