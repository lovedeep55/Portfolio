Mechanical design of L040 motor controller

Project Overview

The Exro Coil Driver is an adaptive electric vehicle traction inverter that seamlessly enables two operating modes in a single electric motor, mitigating the conventional compromise between low-speed torque and high-speed power and efficiency. The Coil Driver leverages patented coil switching technology to boost torque at low speeds and improve power and efficiency at high speeds

Responsibilities

  • Developed detailed CAD models and drawings for the cast heat sink, over-molded plastics, bus bars, and assembly components, incorporating GD&T standards.
  • Led the delivery of the production-version design, working closely with Chinese suppliers to resolve manufacturing challenges.
  • Managed documentation related to Engineering Change Requests (ECRs), Bill of Materials (BOM), and work instructions.
  • Conducted thermal and vibration simulations to address design challenges.
  • Performed Design Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (DFMEA), Root Cause Analysis (RCA), and Design Verification Plan & Report (DVP&R) testing in compliance with ISO standards.

Manufacturing tolerance related issues

  • Situation:
    • The first batch of heat sinks received from the supplier did not meet the required tolerances. Additionally, some holes were misaligned by 1mm.
    • There was also a porosity issue with the cast heat sink, affecting structural integrity and performance.
  • Task:
    • As the mechanical engineer for this project, I was responsible for identifying and resolving manufacturing issues related to the cast heat sinks.
  • Action:
    • The first step was to analyze the root cause of the 1mm positional shift in the heat sink holes.
    • Upon investigation, I identified that the issue stemmed from Geometric Dimensioning & Tolerancing (GD&T) datum references.
    • The existing drawings used a single set of datums, which effectively controlled machined features but failed to control the as-cast features accurately.
    • To address this, I implemented a dual-datum system:
      1. The first datum set controlled cast features.
      2. The second datum set, based on the first, controlled machined features.
  • Result:
    • The revised dual-datum approach allowed for looser tolerances on cast features, eliminating the need for additional machining operations.
    • This change reduced production failures from 7 out of 10 to 2 out of 10.
    • The new tolerance strategy led to an estimated cost reduction of 5-7% by minimizing stringent machining requirements on cast features.

Cast heat sink material change

  • Situation:
    • Due to porosity issues and the high cost of the original material (ADC1), a collective decision was made to evaluate an alternative material within the ADC family.
    • I was given two weeks to assess the performance of the new material compared to ADC1.
  • Task:
    • I was tasked to evaluate this new material based on thermal, mechanical and manufacturing quailuty with ADC1
  • Action:
    • Upon receiving the first article inspection report and samples from the supplier, I conducted a detailed evaluation of the new cast heat sink.
    • The inspection process included:
      1. Visual inspection for surface quality.
      1. Fitment testing with other components.
      1. X-ray analysis to assess porosity levels.
      1. CMM (Coordinate Measuring Machine) analysis for dimensional accuracy.
    • A thermal conductivity analysis revealed that the new material had a 10 W/m·K lower thermal conductivity compared to ADC1, indicating inferior thermal performance.
    • To quantify the impact, I conducted an experiment using resistors as equivalent heat sources, comparing both heat sinks under identical environmental conditions and load profiles.
  • Result:
    • The new heat sink exhibited a 2°C temperature increase under the same operating conditions compared to ADC1.
    • However, the new material reduced material costs by 8%.
    • Additionally, porosity defects were significantly lower, with only 1 in 10 samples affected, compared to 7 in 10 for ADC1.

Capacitor pin breaking in Vibration testing

  • Situation:
    • During vibration simulations of the L040 motor controller (conducted per ISO 16750), I observed that the capacitor pins were breaking under stress.
    • The team proceeded with physical testing, and the unit failed at the exact location predicted in the simulations.
  • Task:
    • Following the failure, I was assigned the task of identifying a quick and effective solution to mitigate capacitor pin breakage.
  • Action:
    • I conducted baseline simulations to further analyze the failure mechanism.
    • It became evident that energy transfer to the capacitor was causing pin deflection, leading to fatigue failure.
    • To address this, I applied an off-the-shelf RTV (Room Temperature Vulcanizing) silicone at the base of the capacitor and re-ran the simulations.
  • Result:
    • The revised design with RTV silicone reinforcement successfully passed vibration testing, ensuring improved durability of the DC-line board.
